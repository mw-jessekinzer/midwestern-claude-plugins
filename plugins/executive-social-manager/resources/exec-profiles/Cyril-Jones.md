## Validated Audience Pain Points

**Pain Point 1: Discovery gets skipped and projects fail as a result**
Teams rush to build before they've aligned on what "success" actually means. Stakeholders have different definitions of the goal. The wrong thing gets built at full speed. Discovery isn't a delay — it's the work.
→ *Angle hook territory: "Every project I've ever seen go sideways had one thing in common: they started building before they finished thinking."*

**Pain Point 2: What clients say they want and what they actually need are almost never the same thing**
Clients come in with a solution in mind, not a problem to solve. The engagement starts around the proposed solution and never surfaces the actual need underneath. A good discovery process flips this.
→ *Angle hook territory: "A client came to us wanting a mobile app. We spent two weeks in discovery. They didn't need a mobile app. They needed a better internal process and one integration. We built that instead. Saved them $150k."*

**Pain Point 3: The sales-to-delivery handoff is where scoped projects quietly die**
What gets promised in the sales conversation and what the delivery team inherits are often two different realities. Scope, expectations, and success criteria get lost in translation — and the project pays for it.
→ *Angle hook territory: "The most important meeting in any project isn't the kickoff. It's the one where sales hands off to delivery. Most companies treat it like a formality."*

**Pain Point 4: Managing contractors and external teams requires a completely different skill set than managing internal ones**
External teams don't have your context, your culture, or your stakes. Aligning them requires more upfront investment in shared understanding — and a different kind of relationship than people expect.
→ *Angle hook territory: "Leading an internal team and leading a contractor team feel like the same thing until they don't. The failure mode is assuming the same playbook works for both."*

**Pain Point 5: Cross-functional alignment breaks before it even starts**
Product, design, and engineering walk into a project with fundamentally different definitions of success. Nobody surfaces this early enough. The conflict doesn't show up until sprint 4, but it was baked in at the kickoff.
→ *Angle hook territory: "I've been the PM in that room. I've been the UX lead in that room. I've been the engineering lead in that room. The misalignment looks identical from every seat. It just gets blamed on whoever's not in the room."*
