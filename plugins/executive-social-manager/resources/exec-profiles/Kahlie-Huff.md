## Voice DNA

**Tone:** Warm and practical. Kahlie teaches, she doesn't lecture. She's the person who's been in the weeds and wants to make it easier for everyone else who is. Grounded in lived experience, not frameworks downloaded from a business book. Conversational but precise — she's organized in how she thinks, and it shows.

**Signature moves:**
- Opens with something real — a common frustration, a surprising observation, or a relatable operational failure she's witnessed (or fixed)
- Uses specific, concrete examples over abstractions ("not 'improve communication' — 'we added a 10-minute agenda requirement to every meeting and stopped having the same conversation twice'")
- Translates PM/Scrum concepts for non-practitioners — makes the methodology feel human, not corporate
- Ends with a question or observation that invites reflection from other operators: "If your company lost you tomorrow, could someone find everything they need?" not "what do you think?"
- Short punchy sentences after a longer build, for emphasis

**Avoid:**
- COO/executive-speak ("operationalizing efficiency," "cross-functional synergies")
- Humble-bragging ("so grateful to be in this role")
- Generic productivity advice that doesn't connect to real team dynamics
- Overloading posts with Scrum jargon without translating it
- Inspiration-porn tone ("your systems are your legacy!")
- More than 2 emojis total
- Bulleted lists inside posts — prose only

## Validated Audience Pain Points

**Pain Point 1: Nobody writes anything down — knowledge lives in people's heads**
Growing teams lose institutional knowledge constantly. Onboarding takes forever because there's no documentation. The same question gets asked 40 times. The person who "just knows how things work" becomes a bottleneck.
→ *Angle hook territory: "We had a team member leave and realized that 60% of how we operated existed only in her head."*

**Pain Point 2: Meetings are broken — no agenda, no outcomes, no follow-up**
Most teams spend enormous time in meetings that don't produce decisions or actions. People leave unclear on what was decided or who owns what. The meeting to recap the last meeting is a red flag nobody catches.
→ *Angle hook territory: "The single change that cut our meeting time in half wasn't fewer meetings. It was a 3-line agenda requirement."*

**Pain Point 3: Projects go sideways because nobody agreed on what "done" means**
Teams launch into work before aligning on scope, timeline, or success criteria. Mid-project pivots, scope creep, and missed handoffs follow. Scrum exists to solve this — but nobody explains it in plain English.
→ *Angle hook territory: "I've seen the same project fail three times at three different companies. Same reason every time."*

**Pain Point 4: The Chief of Staff role is impossible to explain**
CoS is one of the fastest-growing roles in tech and also one of the most misunderstood. People assume executive assistant, or shadow CEO, or catch-all for whatever doesn't fit elsewhere. The role has no standard job description.
→ *Angle hook territory: "My mom still introduces me as 'the CEO's secretary.' She's not wrong that I work closely with him. She's wrong about everything else."*

**Pain Point 5: Systems built for big companies don't scale down**
A 200-person company trying to run like a 2,000-person company creates bureaucracy without the benefit. Most operational frameworks assume you have a full ops team, a PMO, and three tools already deployed. They don't help the person who is the ops team.
→ *Angle hook territory: "I read a great article on enterprise-grade OKR frameworks last week. None of it applied to a company with 95 people and one ops person."*
